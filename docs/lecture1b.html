Three demos on a ball throw — and the two failures that motivate the rest of the school

A three-demo staircase

One simulator, one observation, three algorithms. Each demo fixes a specific failure of the previous one — until we hit two failures that no classical method fixes. Those two are why the rest of the school exists.

  1. Rejection ABC. Establishes the method. Sample efficiency is brutal.
  2. + Summary statistic. A good summary buys orders of magnitude. But who chooses it?
  3. KDE in (\(\theta, x\))-space. Uses all simulations, no \(\varepsilon\) to tune. Dies in high dimensions.
The two failures we will name at the end:
  • Summary identification when \(x\) is structured / high-D  →  neural summary networks (Lec 4).
  • Density estimation in high D — KDE collapses  →  normalising flows, NPE / NLE (Lec 2–3).

What is Approximate Bayesian Computation?

Lec 1a's closing slide: when we cannot write \(p(x\mid\theta)\) but we can run the simulator, the simplest possible inference loop is

for i in 1..N:
    θ_i ∼ p(θ)                     # draw from the prior
    x_i  ∼ p(x | θ_i)              # run the simulator
    if d(x_i, x_obs) < ε:           # accept if close enough
        keep θ_i

The kept \(\theta\)'s are samples from an approximation to \(p(\theta\mid x_\mathrm{obs})\) — exact only in the limit \(\varepsilon \to 0\), \(N \to \infty\).

  • Three knobs: prior, simulator, distance \(d\) and tolerance \(\varepsilon\).
  • For the live demos we replace \(\varepsilon\) by "keep top-\(M\) closest of \(N\)". Same idea, never an empty plot.

Rubin 1984 (the loop on paper); Pritchard et al. 1999 (the name "ABC"); Sisson, Fan & Beaumont 2018 (Handbook of ABC).

Running example — one-parameter ball throw

  • Parameter: launch angle \(\theta\) (rad), uniform prior on \([0.05,\,\tfrac{\pi}{2}-0.05]\).
  • Simulator: projectile, \(v_0 = 10\,\mathrm{m/s}\), \(g = 9.81\). Landing position \[ x = \frac{v_0^2}{g}\sin(2\theta) + \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2),\quad \sigma = 0.30. \]
  • Observation: one \(x_\mathrm{obs}\), generated once from \(\theta_\mathrm{true} = 0.60\) rad.
  • Why 1D/1D: the posterior can be plotted live and overlaid on the analytic truth.
  • \(\sin(2\theta)\) is symmetric around \(\theta=\pi/4\): \(\theta\mapsto r(\theta)\) is two-to-one, so the posterior is bimodal. Every demo below should recover a two-peaked red curve.

Demo 1 — Rejection ABC

Left: \(N\) prior draws (grey) and the top-\(M\) kept (orange) by distance to \(x_\mathrm{obs}\). Right: histogram of accepted \(\theta\) vs reference posterior.

Demo 1 — what just happened

Small \(M\) → noisy histogram. Two real peaks drown in Monte Carlo noise.
Large \(M\) (near \(N\)) → "accept everything"; the histogram leaks back toward the prior.
Small \(N\) → very few sims near \(x_\mathrm{obs}\); even the top-\(M\) are far. Wasted simulator calls.

To sharpen the posterior we must shrink the effective acceptance radius. To shrink it we must throw more sims. To throw more sims we burn the simulator budget. This is the sample-efficiency cliff.

Open question. Each retained sample uses 1 simulator call. The thousands of rejected ones contribute nothing. Is there a way to use them?

Beaumont, Zhang & Balding 2002; Marin et al. 2012, "Approximate Bayesian Computational methods," Stat. Comput. 22.

Demo 2 — Add a summary statistic

Same algorithm; the summary is the mean of \(n_\mathrm{balls}\) throws per \(\theta\). Left: \(n_\mathrm{balls}=1\) (= Demo 1). Right: current slider value. The reference curve also tightens because the effective likelihood is \(\mathcal N(x_\mathrm{obs}; r(\theta), \sigma^2/n_\mathrm{balls})\).

Demo 2 — what just happened

  • Variance of the summary scales as \(\sigma^2/n_\mathrm{balls}\). The effective acceptance radius shrinks at fixed \(M/N\).
  • At the same simulator budget (\(N\) calls — counting each ball as one), a well-chosen summary buys orders of magnitude in sample efficiency.
  • The posterior sharpens past the truth as \(n_\mathrm{balls}\) grows, because the data are more informative. This is correct, not a bug.
Open question (the one that does not go away). Here we chose the summary by hand: "take the mean." Easy, because the simulator is one-dimensional and we know what is informative.

When \(x\) is a 256×256 image, a waveform, a sky map, a graph — which function of \(x\) should we condition on? Hand-engineered summaries are how classical SBI dies.

Fearnhead & Prangle 2012 (semi-automatic summaries); Joyce & Marjoram 2008 (sufficiency in ABC); Chen et al. 2023 (review of neural summaries).

Demo 3 — KDE in \((\theta, x)\)-space

No accept/reject. Build a Gaussian KDE on all \(N\) pairs \((\theta_i, x_i)\), draw a vertical slice at \(x_\mathrm{obs}\) (dashed line, left), renormalise over \(\theta\) — that is the posterior (right).

Demo 3 — the \(\varepsilon \to 0\) limit done right

  • Uses all \(N\) simulations. No rejected sims.
  • No \(\varepsilon\) to tune. The bandwidth \(h\) plays a related role, but it is chosen automatically (Scott's rule, here scaled by the slider).
  • At the same \(N\) as Demo 1, the bimodal structure is recovered cleanly instead of histogrammed-against.
Conceptually: we are modelling the joint density \(p(\theta, x)\) on the simulation cloud, then conditioning at \(x_\mathrm{obs}\). Bayes' theorem on a density model. This is the seed idea of every neural-SBI method that follows: replace ABC's accept/reject by a learned density.

So why is this not the end of the school?

Blum 2010, "Approximate Bayesian Computation: a nonparametric perspective," JASA 105; Silverman 1986 (KDE).

Demo 3b — what kills the KDE

Each added \(x\)-component is pure Gaussian noise, independent of \(\theta\) — it carries zero information. The reference posterior does not change. But the KDE estimate degrades rapidly.

Two failures — the school in one figure

Failure 1 — Summary identification.

When \(x\) is high-dimensional, structured, or just unobvious, which function of \(x\) should we use? Hand-picking does not scale.

→ Neural summary / embedding networks. Lecture 4.

Failure 2 — Density estimation in high D.

Even with a good summary, KDE's bandwidth-per-dimension argument falls apart: the curse of dimensionality.

→ Normalising flows, conditional density estimators, NPE / NLE. Lectures 2–3.

The neural bridge. Replace KDE's product-of-Gaussian-bumps by a learned conditional density \(q_\phi(\theta\mid x)\) — a neural network whose inputs are summary features of \(x\) (also learned) and whose outputs parameterise a flexible distribution over \(\theta\). One network, trained end-to-end on simulator output, attacks both failures at once.

The rest of the school builds this network, piece by piece.

Where we are, where we are going

Today, in one line each

  • Rejection ABC works in principle, drowns in Monte Carlo cost in practice.
  • Summaries rescue sample efficiency — when we know what to summarise.
  • KDE on the joint cloud uses all sims, has no \(\varepsilon\), and points straight at neural conditional density estimation.
  • Two named failures: summary identification and high-D density estimation.
