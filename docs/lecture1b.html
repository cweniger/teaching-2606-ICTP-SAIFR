Running example — a cannon on flat ground

A cannon fires a ball at launch angle \(\theta\), fixed speed \(v_0 = 10\) m/s, gravity \(g = 9.81\). We see only the noisy landing position \(x\).

Inference question: given one observed \(x_\mathrm{obs}\), what was \(\theta\)?

Why this example. The map \(\theta \mapsto x\) is two-to-one: \(\theta\) and \(\tfrac{\pi}{2}-\theta\) land at the same expected position. The posterior is generically bimodal. Move the slider.

The simulator, written down

  • Parameter: \(\theta \in [0.05,\,\tfrac{\pi}{2}-0.05]\) rad, uniform prior.
  • Forward model: \[ x = \underbrace{\frac{v_0^2}{g}\sin(2\theta)}_{r(\theta)} + \varepsilon,\quad \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2),\quad \sigma = 0.30\,\mathrm{m}. \]
  • Observation: one \(x_\mathrm{obs}\), generated once from \(\theta_\mathrm{true} = 0.60\) rad.
  • Closed-form likelihood: \(p(x\mid\theta) = \mathcal{N}(x;\, r(\theta),\, \sigma^2)\). For ABC we will not use it — only sample from the simulator. The reference posterior (right, fine grid) is our benchmark.

Reference notebook: notebooks/lec1b_abc_demos.ipynb.

Approximate Bayesian Computation — the loop

When we can run the simulator but cannot or do not want to write down \(p(x\mid\theta)\), the simplest possible inference loop is:

for i in 1..N:
    θ_i ∼ p(θ)                     # draw from the prior
    x_i  ∼ p(x | θ_i)              # run the simulator
    if d(x_i, x_obs) < ε:           # accept if close enough
        keep θ_i

The kept \(\theta\)'s are samples from an approximation to \(p(\theta\mid x_\mathrm{obs})\) — exact only in the limit \(\varepsilon \to 0\), \(N \to \infty\).

  • Three knobs: prior, simulator, distance \(d\) and tolerance \(\varepsilon\).
  • For the live demos we replace \(\varepsilon\) by "keep top-\(M\) closest of \(N\)". Same idea, never an empty plot.

Rubin 1984 (the loop on paper); Pritchard et al. 1999 (the name "ABC"); Sisson, Fan & Beaumont 2018 (Handbook of ABC).

Demo 1 — Rejection ABC

Left: \(N\) prior draws (grey) and the top-\(M\) kept (orange) by distance to \(x_\mathrm{obs}\). Right: histogram of accepted \(\theta\) vs reference posterior. The readout reports \(\varepsilon_\mathrm{eff} = \max_{i \in \mathrm{kept}} |x_i - x_\mathrm{obs}|\).

Demo 1 — what just happened

  • Small \(M\) → noisy histogram. The two real peaks drown in Monte Carlo noise.
  • Large \(M\) (near \(N\)) → "accept everything"; the histogram leaks back toward the prior.
  • Small \(N\) → very few sims land near \(x_\mathrm{obs}\); even the top-\(M\) are far. Wasted simulator calls.
  • To sharpen the posterior we must shrink \(\varepsilon_\mathrm{eff}\). To shrink it we must throw more sims. This is the sample-efficiency cliff.
Open question. Each retained sample uses one simulator call. The thousands of rejected ones contribute nothing. Is there a way to use them?

Beaumont, Zhang & Balding 2002; Marin et al. 2012, "Approximate Bayesian Computational methods," Stat. Comput. 22.

Setting up Demo 2 — many throws, one angle

What if we throw the ball \(n_\mathrm{balls}\) times at the same launch angle \(\theta\) and observe \(n_\mathrm{balls}\) landing positions \((x_1, \dots, x_{n_\mathrm{balls}})\)?

Each landing carries its own measurement noise. A natural summary is the mean:

\[ s(x_1,\dots,x_{n_\mathrm{balls}}) \;=\; \frac{1}{n_\mathrm{balls}} \sum_{k=1}^{n_\mathrm{balls}} x_k \]

Its variance shrinks by \(1/n_\mathrm{balls}\): the summary is a sharper observation of the same angle.

Demo 2 — ABC with a summary statistic

Toggle off: summary is the raw single landing position (= Demo 1, \(n_\mathrm{balls}\) ignored). Toggle on: summary is the mean of \(n_\mathrm{balls}\) landings per \(\theta\), and the reference shrinks because the effective likelihood is \(\mathcal N(x_\mathrm{obs};\, r(\theta),\, \sigma^2/n_\mathrm{balls})\).

Demo 2 — what just happened

  • Variance of the summary scales as \(\sigma^2/n_\mathrm{balls}\). The effective acceptance radius \(\varepsilon_\mathrm{eff}\) shrinks at fixed \(M/N\).
  • At the same simulator budget (counting each ball as one call), a well-chosen summary buys orders of magnitude in sample efficiency.
  • The posterior sharpens past the truth as \(n_\mathrm{balls}\) grows, because the data are more informative. This is correct, not a bug.
Open question (the one that does not go away). Here we chose the summary by hand: "take the mean." Easy, because the simulator is one-dimensional and we know what is informative.

When \(x\) is a 256×256 image, a waveform, a sky map, a graph — which function of \(x\) should we condition on? Hand-engineered summaries are how classical SBI dies.

Fearnhead & Prangle 2012 (semi-automatic summaries); Joyce & Marjoram 2008 (sufficiency in ABC); Chen et al. 2023 (review of neural summaries).

Demo 3 — KDE in \((\theta, x)\)-space

Left: Gaussian KDE contours on \(p(\theta, x)\) over all \(N\) sims, with \(x_\mathrm{obs}\) marked. Right: weighted histogram \(w_i = K_{h_x}(x_\mathrm{obs}-x_i)\) and its smoothed KDE slice, vs the reference posterior. No accept/reject, no \(\varepsilon\).

Demo 3 — the \(\varepsilon \to 0\) limit done right

  • Uses all \(N\) simulations — nothing rejected.
  • No \(\varepsilon\) to tune. The bandwidth \(h_x\) plays a related role but is chosen automatically; for this 1D simulator, \(h_x \approx \sigma\) (the noise level) is the right answer.
  • At the same \(N\) as Demo 1, the bimodal structure is recovered cleanly instead of histogrammed-against.
Conceptually: we are modelling the joint density \(p(\theta, x)\) on the simulation cloud, then conditioning at \(x_\mathrm{obs}\). Bayes' theorem on a density model. This is the seed idea of every neural-SBI method that follows: replace ABC's accept/reject by a learned density.

Blum 2010, "Approximate Bayesian Computation: a nonparametric perspective," JASA 105; Silverman 1986 (KDE).

MCMC vs amortised ABC — same answer, different cost

MCMC (likelihood-based). Use the explicit \(\mathcal{L}(\theta)=\mathcal{N}(x_\mathrm{obs}; r(\theta),\sigma^2)\). For every new \(x_\mathrm{obs}\) we must re-run a chain from scratch.
Amortised KDE (joint-density). Estimate \(p(\theta, x)\) once from 2000 sims, then any new \(x_\mathrm{obs}\) is a free slice. Cost paid up front.

Three demos, in one breath — and what comes next

The staircase, in retrospect

  1. Rejection ABC. Works in principle, drowns in Monte Carlo cost in practice.
  2. + Summary statistic. Rescues sample efficiency — when we know what to summarise.
  3. KDE on \((\theta, x)\). Uses every sim, no \(\varepsilon\), and is amortised: pay once, infer for any \(x_\mathrm{obs}\).

The conceptual jump — from "filter samples" to "model the joint density" — is the seed of every neural SBI method that follows.

Where classical ABC dies, and where we go

Hand-picked summaries don't scale when \(x\) is an image, a waveform, a graph. → Neural summary / embedding networks. Lecture 4.
KDE on the joint dies in high D. A kernel cannot model \(p(\theta, x)\) when \(\dim\theta\) or \(\dim x\) is large. → Replace KDE by a learned conditional density \(q_\phi(\theta\mid x)\). Lectures 2–3.

Classical ABC is the rejection-sampling skeleton of the whole field. Everything we add from here is a way to spend simulator calls more wisely.
